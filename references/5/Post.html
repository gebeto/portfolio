Measuring cold starts the honest way

P99 latency on serverless platforms is a beautiful lie. It hides the number that actually matters — the one your first user hits at 7:03 on a Monday morning. Here's the small harness I use to find it, and why it changed how I size functions.

by Yaroslav Nychkalo

Every team I've worked with has, at some point, looked at a dashboard showing a flat 40 ms P99 line and concluded that their serverless setup is fine. It isn't. That line is the shape of your warm traffic — the requests that found an instance already running, already connected to the database, already happy. It tells you almost nothing about the experience of the first user to land on a quiet endpoint after it's been idle for fifteen minutes.

The honest measurement of a cold start isn't a percentile — it's a cohort. Specifically: the distribution of latencies observed on requests where no warm instance was available at the moment of dispatch. Most observability tooling won't give you this cohort directly, because the platform doesn't expose it. You have to synthesize it.

The harness

For the last few months I've been running the same tiny harness against every serverless endpoint I ship. It's about eighty lines of Go and does exactly one thing: it waits long enough to guarantee a cold start, fires a single request, records the wall-clock latency, and waits again. No parallelism, no P99, no averaging. Just a stream of honest numbers. 

for i := 0; i < n; i++ {
    time.Sleep(coldWindow)   // longer than the platform's keep-alive
    start := time.Now()
    _, err := http.Get(url)
    latency := time.Since(start)
    record(latency, err)
}

The only clever bit is coldWindow. For AWS Lambda I use eighteen minutes. For Cloud Run, twelve. For Vercel's Edge Functions, about five. These numbers aren't in the docs — I found them by running the harness at different intervals until the latency distribution collapsed into two obvious modes.

What the numbers looked like

On the routing service I'm rewriting at SPSoft, the warm P99 was 38 ms. The cold-start cohort — what the first user of the morning actually experienced — was a median of 1.9 seconds, with a long tail out to four. That's a fiftyfold gap that no dashboard was showing us.

Three things followed from knowing that number:

  1. We stopped apologizing for the 40 ms P99 and started designing around the 1.9 s median cold.
  2. We moved the hottest endpoints to a small container that never sleeps, and kept the cheap ones on Lambda with a clear SLO for first-hit latency.
  3. We added a synthetic prober that fires a request every ten minutes to the three endpoints customers land on most often. Not because the harness wasn't working — but because we now cared enough to keep them warm.

Why P99 lies

P99 is a population statistic. Cold starts are, by definition, a minority event — often less than half a percent of your traffic, because most real traffic hits warm instances. The cold requests vanish into the tail of the distribution, and then the tail gets trimmed because "we don't care about the worst 1%."

The user doesn't experience percentiles. They experience the single request they made.

If your traffic is bursty — and everyone's traffic is bursty — a significant fraction of real users are drawn from that bottom 1%. They're the ones opening your app first thing in the morning, or after lunch, or the moment a marketing email goes out. Treating them as statistical noise is a choice. It's just not a choice most teams are making deliberately.

What to do with the number

Once you have an honest cold-start median, a few things get easier:

  • Sizing decisions. If cold is 2 seconds and your SLA is 500 ms, you need warm instances. No amount of clever code paths fixes two orders of magnitude.
  • Runtime choices. A Go binary on Lambda is a very different number than a Node process with thirty megabytes of dependencies. The harness lets you make the comparison without ceremony.
  • Product conversations. "The first request of the morning takes two seconds" is a sentence a product manager can reason about. "P99 is 40 ms but P99.9 is 1.8 seconds" is a sentence that gets nodded at and forgotten.

The code is in gebeto/cold-honest if you want to steal it. It's small enough that you should probably rewrite it anyway — the point isn't the tool, it's asking a different question.

The next time someone shows you a 40 ms P99 and calls it a day, ask them what the first user of the morning saw. If they don't know, neither do you.
